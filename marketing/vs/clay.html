Weft vs Clay

Two personal CRMs with opposite design philosophies. Clay treats AI as a feature; Weft treats AI as something to refuse. Pick the one whose stance matches yours.

The one-line answer. Clay is a beautiful Mac-first personal CRM with heavy AI summarization and a premium price. Weft is a quiet iOS-first indie with zero cloud AI, written by one person, priced for a single individual paying out of pocket.

What each tool is designed for

Clay is a polished, design-forward product (clay.earth) that bills itself as "the operating system for your personal life." The defining feature is its AI summarization: pull in emails, calendar events, and contacts from many sources, let the cloud LLM write paragraph summaries of each person and surface "you should reach out" prompts. The aesthetic is gorgeous — soft palettes, careful typography. The price is correspondingly premium ($120+/year as of 2026), and the company is venture-funded.

Weft is the small-studio counterpoint. The app is built by one developer in Tokyo, runs only on iOS 26, and has no AI features at all. There is no cloud LLM in the picture — full stop. The product believes that the value is in you writing the sentence about someone; an auto-generated summary doesn't actually help you remember them, it just makes the database look populated.

Where Clay is genuinely better

If you want the relationship layer over your existing communication tools — Gmail, calendar, conference contacts — Clay does this beautifully. The AI summarization, for what it is, can save real time if you trust it. The macOS app is genuinely lovely. If you live on a Mac and want a polished, full-featured personal CRM that touches all your channels, Clay is mature and well-designed.

If you actively want AI to do the remembering for you, Weft will not serve that need.

Where Weft is genuinely better

If you've recoiled at the idea of an LLM reading every email about your close friends, Weft is built for you. There is no cloud LLM in Weft. There is no Weft server. There is no "we'd never look at your data" promise — there's no data to look at, because none of it leaves your phone unless you opt into Apple's end-to-end-encrypted iCloud, and even then we still can't see it.

If you're paying for an indie app out of pocket (no expense account), Weft costs about a fifth of Clay. If you live on iOS rather than macOS, Weft's home-screen and lock-screen widgets fit the device you already use most. If you find auto-generated summaries vaguely creepy — like reading a CRM's polite description of a friend — Weft's blank textarea is calmer.

And honestly: most people don't have a thousand close friends. They have ten. The "OS for your personal life" framing assumes a busier life than most of us have. Weft assumes the opposite.

The honest verdict

The question that distinguishes these two:

  • "Do I want AI to read my emails and tell me about my friends?" → Clay, if yes.
  • "Do I want a quiet, on-device notebook where I do the remembering myself?" → Weft, if yes.

Both are valid answers. They just point to different products.
