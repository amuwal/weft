Weft vs Notion personal CRM templates

Tried building a personal CRM in Notion and bounced off? You're not alone. Here's why a focused iOS app outperforms a template for remembering the small circle of people you care about.

The one-line answer. Notion is a powerful database that can be configured into anything. Weft is a small, focused app that's already configured to do exactly one thing well. Templates win on flexibility; focused apps win on follow-through.

The Notion personal CRM problem

Search "Notion personal CRM template" and you'll find dozens of beautifully-built templates — sortable contacts databases, last-contact-date columns, follow-up reminders, sometimes even integrations via Make or Zapier. The templates are real, often well-designed, and sometimes free.

They also share an unspoken weakness: the average user gives up on them within three weeks.

The reason isn't a Notion failing — it's a category failing. A personal CRM in Notion requires you to:

  • Open a laptop or unlock a phone, then open Notion, then navigate to a workspace, then a page, then a database view (5+ taps before you can write the sentence)
  • Configure the database properties to your needs (relationship type, rhythm, last contact date)
  • Maintain the configuration as your needs change
  • Decide every time whether to add someone, what columns to fill in, which view to use
  • Remember to open Notion when you remember the small thing you wanted to record

The gap between "I should write a note about that" and the moment Notion is open is usually long enough to forget. Notion is built for desk work. The act of caring about a friend mostly happens away from the desk.

What a focused app does instead

Weft inverts most of those decisions:

  • One tap from your home screen. No workspace, no page, no database. The Today screen surfaces 0–3 people automatically.
  • Pre-decided fields. Name, relationship type, rhythm. No column configuration. You can't get it wrong because there's no decision to make.
  • Pre-decided vocabulary. Notes, threads, rhythm. No tagging schemes to invent.
  • Apple Watch + lock-screen widget so the cue to write a note can reach you before the laptop does.
  • Designed for the gap between meetings and bedtime, on the device you actually have on you. Not for "block out an hour to update your personal CRM."

The trade you make in exchange for that focus is flexibility. Weft cannot become anything else. It cannot also be your habit tracker, your reading list, or your project plan. That's by design — the people you love deserve their own surface, not a tab in a busier database.

When Notion is actually the right answer

If you already live in Notion all day for work, the marginal effort to add a contacts database is small. If you want one workspace that holds reading lists, project notes, AND people notes, Notion's flexibility is the win. If your day is mostly at a desk and you don't mind the open-the-laptop friction, the template works.

And if you've successfully maintained a Notion personal CRM for more than six months, you genuinely don't need Weft. Stay with what works.

When Weft is the right answer

If you've started a Notion personal CRM database three times and abandoned it three times — most people have — the problem isn't your willpower. The problem is that the tool's shape is wrong for the job. Weft is shaped for the job:

  • Always on your wrist or in your pocket
  • One tap from the home screen to the right view
  • No setup decisions to revisit
  • A daily Today surface that does the "who should I think about" work for you
  • A small intentional cap on people, so the database can't bloat into uselessness
  • $18.99 a year (or $39.99 once for a lifetime purchase) — less than two months of Notion Plus

The honest verdict

  • "I want a customizable database where one tab is my personal CRM among many other things." → Notion + a template.
  • "I want a focused app that I'll actually open every day for two seconds, on my phone." → Weft.

The single biggest predictor of whether a personal CRM works is whether you actually open it. Apps with low friction win on consistency. That's the only thing that matters in the end — the database with five entries you maintain is more valuable than the database with five hundred entries you abandoned.
